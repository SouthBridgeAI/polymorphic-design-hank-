# Story Review: "Not Yet"

**Reviewer's note:** I came to this cold. I read the spec without context, then read the creative brief and merge decisions after forming my own impression. This review is of the story as it exists in Part Four of the production spec.

---

## Narrative Flow

### What works

The three-story weave is the comic's structural triumph. The progression is:

- **Strip 1:** Asimov ("The Last Question") — the question is born
- **Strip 2:** Clarke ("Nine Billion Names") — a different question, same patience
- **Strip 3:** Clarke — the work of naming
- **Strip 4:** Asimov — the family, the child, tenderness
- **Strip 5:** Hankweave — the 3 AM pipeline, fourth wall
- **Strip 6:** Asimov — deep future, the question persists
- **Strip 7:** Clarke — the brink of completion
- **Strip 8:** Asimov — the universe dims
- **Strip 9:** Clarke — the stars go out
- **Strip 10:** Crisis — Epoch alone in the void
- **Strip 11:** Climax — reversal, LET THERE BE LIGHT
- **Strip 12:** Resolution — morning, the pipeline finished

This interleaving is smart. It prevents either Asimov or Clarke's plotline from going stale, and the Hankweave strip (5) lands perfectly at the midpoint — a breath of reality between two fictional arcs.

Each strip builds on the last. There are no "wait, what?" moments except one (see below). The reader always knows *where* they are because the settings are so distinct: underground computer chamber, Himalayan monastery, spaceship, laptop, deep space, void.

### What doesn't work

**The Hankweave thread is too thin.** Strip 5 is excellent. Strip 12 is excellent. But Strip 5 is the *only* Hankweave-dedicated strip in the middle of the comic. The fourth-wall thread — the developer, the pipeline, the real-world meaning — appears once and then vanishes for six strips until the ending. This makes Strip 12 feel slightly abrupt: the developer's hands reaching for the laptop is a return to a world we barely established.

The merge notes specifically borrowed the Hankweave strip ("The Quiet Codon") from Story A because it was the strongest Hankweave moment. But one strip isn't enough to earn the ending. Strip 12's payoff asks the reader to feel something about the developer's morning — but we spent four panels with that developer total.

**Strip 6 is the weakest strip in the comic.** The disembodied minds (VJ-23X and MQ-17J) are abstract blobs asking abstract questions. There's no character, no tension, no humor, no warmth. It's "The Last Question" on fast-forward — hitting the plot point (the Galactic AC says "not yet" again) without any emotional payload. Epoch "floating in space" feels arbitrary rather than earned. Compare this to Strip 4, where the child crying "Don't let the stars run down!" hits like a hammer — Strip 6 has nothing that visceral.

This strip also creates a chronological confusion. We jump from the modern developer's desk (Strip 5) to the far galactic future (Strip 6) without transition. The reader absorbs the 3 AM loneliness, then is suddenly among luminous gas beings between galaxies. It's the one place the weaving stumbles.

## Emotional Arc

### The arc as a whole

The emotional progression is genuinely well-constructed:

1. **Curiosity** (Strip 1) — a bar argument, a question
2. **Awe** (Strip 2) — vast landscape, spiritual recognition
3. **Devotion** (Strip 3) — sacred mundane, patient work
4. **Tenderness** (Strip 4) — a child's fear, a child's promise
5. **Solitude** (Strip 5) — the 3 AM vigil, faith without witness
6. ~~**Scale** (Strip 6) — cosmic repetition~~ (This is where the arc goes flat. See below.)
7. **Tension** (Strip 7) — the brink, the engineers flee
8. **Dread** (Strip 8) — entropy, dimming, "not yet" as weight
9. **Terror-as-beauty** (Strip 9) — the stars go out
10. **Despair** (Strip 10) — Epoch stops, withdraws
11. **Catharsis** (Strip 11) — the reversal, creation
12. **Peace** (Strip 12) — morning, resolution

This is a real arc. The low point (Strip 10) is earned — it comes after three strips of escalating loss. The resolution (Strip 11) is earned — it's set up by the accumulation of light-threads throughout the comic.

### What falls flat

**Strip 6 is an emotional dead zone.** It sits between the comic's most intimate strip (5: the 3 AM codon) and its most tense act break (7: the engineers leave). It should be doing *something* — building dread, or wonder, or pushing the stakes forward. Instead it's exposition. "The question persists across cosmological time" is information, not feeling. The reader already gets this from the structure itself.

**The resolution in Strip 11 is almost too neat.** The caption "Not because the answer was ready. Because the walking wasn't finished" is the single best line in the comic. But Panel 4 — the full creation moment — goes maximalist in a way that risks undermining the quietness that makes this story work. "LET THERE BE LIGHT!" is Asimov's ending and it's correct to include it. But the visual description (rays, streaming threads, new stars, light flooding everything) may overwhelm the simple fact: a tortoise chose to take one more step, and that was enough.

### What works brilliantly

**Strip 4 is the emotional masterpiece of this comic.** "Don't let them! Don't let the stars run down!" followed by a child tucking a blanket over Epoch's shell, whispering "Don't worry. The Microvac will fix it." Then: "Children make promises on behalf of the universe." This is the strip that earns the ending. Without it, the comic is a clever structural exercise. With it, it's a *story.*

**Strip 10 is essential and well-executed.** The merge notes identified this as the key fix — the moment where patience looks foolish — and the execution delivers. "What if 'not yet' was always 'never'?" is the question every long project asks in the dark. And "Epoch saved this place too" is devastating: even doubt gets checkpointed. That's not just a metaphor — it's *true* about how persistent systems work.

**The "Not yet" repetition is perfect.** It appears in Strips 1, 8, and 10, with "Not yet. Not yet. Not yet. ...Now." closing the comic. This structural motif is the heartbeat. It works.

## Complexity Layering

**Surface level:** A tortoise carries a glowing hourglass through different worlds — a computer room, a mountain monastery, a spaceship, deep space — collecting light until the shell explodes into a new universe. A child tucks the tortoise in. A developer finds the tortoise on their desk in the morning. Adventure, beauty, a happy ending.

**Deeper level:** Three stories about humanity asking machines to answer unanswerable questions. The machine's patience (Multivac's "INSUFFICIENT DATA," the Mark V's name-listing, Hankweave's checkpointing) mirrors the tortoise's patience. The invisible labor of computation — running while no one watches, failing and recovering without credit, accumulating data across incomprehensible timescales — is the labor of anyone who does slow, unglamorous, essential work.

**Deepest level:** "Not yet" is the most honest answer to the hardest question. Not "no." Not "impossible." Not "ask someone else." Just: *not yet.* I'm still working on it. The comic argues that the gap between "not yet" and "yes" is filled by walking — by continuing to work when the answer isn't ready, when the stars are going out, when the pipeline crashes at 3 AM. That gap is where meaning lives.

**Does it avoid being preachy?** Mostly yes. The captions are occasionally too literary — "Children make promises on behalf of the universe" is borderline, but it earns its place because the scene is so specific and tender. "Not every act of faith needs a witness" is the one line that feels like a fortune cookie — it *tells* instead of showing, and the panel already shows it. But overall, the comic trusts its images.

**Would a child enjoy it?** Yes, with caveats. The tortoise is appealing. The child character (Jerrodette I) gives young readers an avatar. The mountain journey, the monastery, the spaceship, the stars going out — all visually compelling. But Strips 6 and 8 are abstract in ways that might lose younger readers. The comic skews older than the stated 7-12 range; I'd say 10+ is more realistic.

**Would an adult find meaning?** Yes. The Hankweave metaphor is specific enough to resonate with anyone who's built systems, run overnight jobs, or done work that nobody notices until it stops working. The Clarke and Asimov references add depth for readers who know the sources, but the story doesn't require that knowledge.

---

## Coherence

**Epoch is consistent.** Patient, quiet, present, accumulating. The one character choice that might confuse: Epoch doesn't *do* anything until Strip 11. The tortoise watches, walks, and carries — that's it. This is thematically correct (the work IS the walking) but it's also the child-reviewer's complaint: "The tortoise walks a lot and doesn't DO anything." The spec addresses this by making the watching *feel* active through the light-thread accumulation, but it's a risk.

**The Lama works.** Appearing in Strips 2, 3, and 7, the Lama serves as the voice of spiritual patience and recognizes Epoch as kindred. Minimal, effective, consistent.

**The Jerrodettes work perfectly.** Strip 4 only. One appearance, unforgettable.

**George and Chuck work.** Two strips (7, 9). They're doubt personified — rational men fleeing a miracle. They work because they're *right to doubt* and *wrong about the outcome.*

**VJ-23X and MQ-17J don't work.** They're props, not characters. Luminous blobs asking a question. They exist solely to advance the Asimov timeline. They have no personality, no stakes, no relationship to Epoch. They're the one place where the comic feels like it's checking a box.

**The Developer barely works.** Two appearances (5, 12), both from a distance. The hands-and-laptop framing is appropriately anonymous, but it means the fourth-wall payoff relies entirely on the *reader* projecting themselves into the scene. This is either bold minimalism or an undercooked character. I lean toward the former, but it's fragile.

## The "Would I Read This?" Test

Yes. I would read all 12 strips. I would slow down at Strip 4 and feel something. I would feel the weight of Strip 10. I would feel the release of Strip 11. I would sit with Strip 12's sunrise.

Would I feel something at the end? Yes. The "Not yet. Not yet. Not yet. ...Now." landing is earned. It would hit me the way good endings do — not as a surprise, but as a recognition.

What would I tell a friend? "It's a comic about a tortoise carrying an hourglass through the end of the universe. It's about two classic sci-fi short stories — Asimov's 'The Last Question' and Clarke's 'The Nine Billion Names of God' — woven together with a story about a software pipeline that runs at 3 AM. It sounds insane. It works."

## Specific Recommendations

### CUT: Strip 6 ("The Galaxies Fill")

This strip doesn't earn its place. It's exposition disguised as cosmic wonder. The disembodied minds are non-characters. The "INSUFFICIENT DATA" answer is repeated from Strip 1 without additional emotional weight. And Epoch floating in space is the one moment where the character feels forced into a scene rather than naturally present.

**What we lose:** The Asimov timeline's middle chapter (the progression from mortal humans to galactic minds).

**What we gain:** Tighter pacing. A harder cut from the intimacy of Strip 5 (3 AM pipeline) directly to the tension of Strip 7 (the engineers leave). The reader gets the passage of time from the context — by the time we reach Strip 8's "The stars were dying," we understand that eons have passed. We don't need the exposition.

**Counter-argument:** The spec wants the Asimov timeline to feel like it spans trillions of years. Without Strip 6, the jumps are: 2061 (Strip 1) → family in hyperspace (Strip 4) → the universe dying (Strip 8). That's only three Asimov beats. Is that enough?

**My answer:** Yes. The three beats are: question asked, question inherited by children, question outlives everything. That's a complete arc. Strip 6 adds information (galactic minds, the AC grows) but not emotion.

### STRENGTHEN: Strip 8 ("The Universe Dims")

With Strip 6 cut, Strip 8 needs to carry more weight as the "deep future" strip. The current version is good but slightly abstract — "Man" as a luminous aura is hard to render emotionally.

**Suggested tweak:** In Panel 1, instead of just the dying stars and the aura, add a faint echo of the child from Strip 4 — not literally Jerrodette, but the caption could reference the promise: "The stars were dying. The children who once cried 'don't let them!' had become part of the question." This ties the cosmic scale back to the emotional anchor of Strip 4 and makes the loss feel personal.

### STRENGTHEN: The Hankweave connection between Strip 5 and Strip 12

The fourth-wall thread is too thin. Two options:

**Option A (Preferred): Add a brief Hankweave echo to Strip 10 or 11.** When Epoch retreats into the shell (Strip 10, Panel 4), the caption "Epoch saved this place too" already uses checkpoint language. Strengthen it: in the visual description for Panel 4 of Strip 10, add a faint terminal-green glow beneath or around the shell — a ghost of the laptop screen from Strip 5. The suggestion: even in the void, the checkpoint persists. This costs nothing in page space and bridges the fourth-wall gap.

**Option B: Add a half-panel terminal insert to Strip 11.** When the data correlates in the shell (Strip 11, Panel 1), the visual description could include a faint overlay of scrolling terminal text within the light-threads — the JSONL logs, the checkpoint data, the pipeline output. The sacred and the computational, visually merged.

### MINOR FIXES

1. **Strip 1, Panel 1:** Adell is described as "the figure on the right" in the spec but "the figure on the left" in the generation prompt. The prompt has it as left, which makes more sense (left-to-right reading: Adell speaks first, from the left). Fix the spec to match the prompt.

2. **Strip 5, Panel 4 caption:** "Not every act of faith needs a witness" — consider softening to let the image speak. The panel already shows faith without a witness; the caption is redundant. But this is a judgment call. The line *is* memorable.

3. **Strip 9, Panel 3:** The spec says "WIDE PANEL" but it's a 2x2 grid — all panels are the same size. The prompt correctly doesn't mention wideness. Remove the "WIDE PANEL" note from the spec or clarify it means the composition should feel wide/panoramic within the standard panel dimensions.

4. **Strip 12, Panel 3:** The figurine ambiguity is the comic's best grace note. Protect it — the prompt handles it well. Don't over-explain it.

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a genuinely good comic story. The three-source weave is ambitious and mostly succeeds. The emotional arc is real. The character works. The ending earns its moment.

Its strongest asset is the contrast between cosmic scale and domestic intimacy — between stars going out and a child tucking in a tortoise, between the universe ending and a laptop completing its pipeline at dawn.

Its weakest point is Strip 6, which drags the middle and dilutes the emotional momentum with abstract exposition. Cut it and the comic tightens from 12 strips to 11, which is sharper.

The second weakness is the thin fourth-wall thread. Strip 5 is the comic's heart, but it's lonely. One more visual echo of the pipeline/developer world somewhere in Act 3 would strengthen the landing.

With those two fixes — one cut, one addition — this is a story I'd want to own as a physical comic. The tortoise, the hourglass, the stars going out, the child's promise, the 3 AM checkpoint, the sunrise. It's all there.

**Rating: 8/10 as-is. 9/10 with the cut and the strengthened Hankweave thread.**
