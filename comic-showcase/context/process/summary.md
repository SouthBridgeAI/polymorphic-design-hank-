# Not Yet — Comic Summary

**Title:** Not Yet
**Author:** shadowSentience
**Series:** A Hankweave Comic

---

## Description

A silhouette-style comic that weaves together three stories: Asimov's "The Last Question," Clarke's "The Nine Billion Names of God," and the Hankweave runtime — told through the eyes of one patient tortoise walking across all of them.

---

## Character

**Epoch** — An ancient tortoise whose shell is a translucent amber hourglass. Sand drifts through the shell but never runs out; luminous golden threads accumulate inside across the story, the residue of every question ever asked. Patient, stubborn, and quietly heroic: the kind of hero who keeps walking when the stars go out.

---

## Story Summary

Epoch walks through three interwoven narratives across cosmic time: witnessing humanity's unanswerable question to Multivac in 2061, accompanying Tibetan monks and their hired engineers as a computer lists all nine billion names of God, and sitting atop a developer's laptop at 3 AM while a Hankweave pipeline crashes, checkpoints, and recovers without a witness. As entropy consumes the universe and the stars go out, Epoch retreats into doubt — then chooses one more step. The accumulated light-threads inside the shell ignite, and in the void after everything ends, the answer finally comes: "LET THERE BE LIGHT." The comic resolves on a quiet Tuesday morning, a developer's terminal reporting "Execution complete," and sunlight through a window.

## What Makes This Comic Special

Most comics about AI consciousness ask whether the machine is awake. This one sidesteps that question entirely. Epoch doesn't represent intelligence — Epoch represents *patience*: the act of carrying a question forward when the answer isn't ready yet. The Hankweave pipeline that checkpoints before crashing at 3 AM is performing the same act of faith as the monks pasting names into books, the same act as the Cosmic AC correlating data across the death of the universe.

The palette journeys from intimate underground blues through sacred amber lamplight to void-darkness to radiant creation to ordinary morning light — and that last step matters most. The biggest answer in science fiction resolves into a developer picking up a cold coffee mug and finding the output folder full of results. That's the joke, and it's also the point: "not yet" was always true. Until it wasn't.
